The research

The core question was straightforward: can we predict where violent extremism will escalate before it does? The original study examined whether kidnapping spikes alone could serve as an early warning signal. The answer, after rigorous testing, was no. Single-type event thresholds do not predict next-week violence when isolated from concurrent attacks. The within-month temporal ordering test showed zero lead: KAFD events do not precede VE attacks within spike-months (Wilcoxon p = 0.68).

But the expanded analysis across 13 countries and 100,184 events revealed a stronger and genuinely predictive signal. When two or more distinct conflict event types co-occur in the same district-week, next-week attacks surge 4-6x above baseline. The signal is not carried by any single event type. It emerges from the convergence of multiple armed group capabilities deployed simultaneously.

Method

Weekly district-level panel across 13 countries (Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, Nigeria, Chad, Cameroon, Mauritania, Togo, Benin, Ghana, Senegal, Guinea, Côte d'Ivoire), 1,503 admin2 districts, January 2010 to March 2025. ACLED event data classified by sub-event type. Composite signal defined as 2+ distinct conflict sub-event types in the same district-week. Next-week attack rates compared using Mann-Whitney U tests. Exclusive signal tests isolate each event type by removing district-weeks with concurrent attacks.

Key findings

1. No single event type predicts next-week violence when isolated. KAFD alone: 0.8x baseline. IED alone: 1.3x. All at or below baseline when concurrent attacks are excluded.

2. Composite co-occurrence is the genuine signal. KAFD + IED co-occurrence: 5.5x next-week attacks, 4.6x fatalities (N=292, p < 0.001). Any 3+ types: 5.5x.

3. Replicates across every region. Central Sahel: 2.6x. Lake Chad Basin: 8.3x. Coastal West Africa: 10.2x. All p < 0.001.

4. Coastal frontier: IEDs in Togo, Benin, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire predict 5.2-7.3x escalating violence over 3 weeks. 28 of 38 district-weeks in 2023-2025.

5. The escalation ladder is driven by convergence of multiple types, not any single type spiking.

The composite co-occurrence signal operates on two levels. First, as a district identifier: districts where KAFD and IED co-occur in the same week are persistently high-conflict, averaging 9.4 times the clean baseline attack rate. Second, as a temporal escalation indicator: within those already-violent districts, the signal week produces a further 1.6-fold spike above the pre-signal corridor. Both dimensions are operationally valuable.

The platform

The research produces a signal. The platform turns that signal into something a protection officer, a programme manager, or a humanitarian coordinator can act on. Three layers, each with a clear function.

Layer 1: Predict

Composite co-occurrence detection from ACLED API data at weekly resolution. The spike detector triggers on convergence of 2+ distinct conflict event sub-types in the same admin2-week. The primary signal is KAFD + IED co-occurrence (5.5x next-week attacks). The output is an escalation alert at admin2 level with a 7-day forward window.

Layer 2: Alert

Signal translation into structured alerts for defined stakeholder groups. Each alert includes severity classification (Watch / Elevated / High / Critical), geographic targeting to the district level, the specific event type combination that triggered the signal, and the regional baseline comparison.

Layer 3: Respond

Decision pathways for different users:

  • Community protection activation for local civil society actors
  • Programme adaptation guidance for P/CVE implementers
  • Pre-positioning recommendations for humanitarian agencies
  • Threat briefing structure for security coordination bodies
Where this stands: Layer 1 is grounded in completed analysis across 100,184 events and 13 countries. The composite co-occurrence signal has been validated with exclusive signal tests, regional replication, and escalation ladder analysis. The ACLED data pipeline for automated weekly ingestion is architecturally specified. The full system architecture is complete.

Methodological foundation

The methodological foundation is composite signal detection at weekly temporal resolution. The analysis pipeline classifies ACLED events by sub-event type and evaluates whether specific combinations co-occurring in the same admin2-week predict elevated violence in the following week. The exclusive signal test strips away concurrent attacks to isolate whether any individual event type carries standalone predictive power. The answer is no: the signal is emergent, arising from the convergence of multiple armed group capabilities.

The Central Sahel was the initial proof of concept. The expanded analysis confirms the signal across 13 countries, with the strongest effects in the Lake Chad Basin and Coastal West Africa.

Wherever ACLED operates, roughly 230 countries and territories, is the addressable scope. The Central Sahel is the proof of concept because that is where I work and where the data is richest for the composite co-occurrence pathway. It is not the ceiling.

Who built this

This was built by a P/CVE practitioner working in the Sahel. I design and manage evaluations of programmes aimed at preventing violent extremism across Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. I have been doing this work for over 12 years, across six countries, and have published 29 peer-reviewed papers on topics ranging from health systems to the climate-conflict nexus. The gap between people who study conflict from university offices and people who work in conflict zones is real, and it shapes what gets built. PRAXIS exists to close that gap. The early warning system comes from the same place as the evaluation tools: someone who needed it, could not find it, and decided to build it.
