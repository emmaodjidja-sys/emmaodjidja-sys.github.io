Two research tracks

Early Warning System

Conflict prediction

PRAXIS applies epidemiological surveillance logic and econometric methods to ACLED event data to predict escalation in violent extremism before it hits. The current research uses a stacked event study design on geocoded data from 2015 to 2023 to show that kidnapping spikes predict subsequent VE surges in the Central Sahel, with spatial spillover quantified at specific radii. The platform translates these signals into actionable alerts for P/CVE programme managers, humanitarian agencies, and security coordination bodies. Five robustness checks confirm the signal across 230+ countries of ACLED coverage.

Programme Evaluation

Evaluation infrastructure

PRAXIS encodes 12 years of field evaluation experience into free, open-source browser tools covering the full evaluation lifecycle. Six live tools, 298 indicators, 11 sectors. Sample size calculation, evaluation design advising, data exploration, Theory of Change building, evaluation matrix construction, and an indicator bank. Every tool runs client-side with zero data transmission. The methodology modules work offline in the places where evaluation work actually happens.

Built by a practitioner

I design and manage evaluations of programmes aimed at preventing violent extremism across Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. I have been doing this work for over 12 years, across six countries, and have published 29 peer-reviewed papers. I serve as section editor for case-based evaluations at the Journal of MultiDisciplinary Evaluation. My training is in epidemiology (MSc Global Health, Distinction, Queen Margaret University Edinburgh), and before working in the Sahel I ran health systems research in South Sudan and Burundi.

The conflict early warning work comes from the same gap that produced the evaluation tools. I needed a way to anticipate where VE would escalate so that programme design could get ahead of events instead of reacting to them. I could not find it, so I built it. The prediction model uses the same econometric rigour I would apply to an impact evaluation. The difference is that instead of measuring what happened, it tries to tell you what is about to.
