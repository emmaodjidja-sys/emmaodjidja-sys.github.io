Featured research

No single event type predicts violence. Co-occurrence does.

Analysis of 100,184 ACLED conflict events across 13 Sahel countries (2010-2025) at weekly district-level resolution. When individual conflict event types occur in isolation, next-week attack rates drop to or below baseline. When two or more types co-occur in the same district-week, lethal violence follows within 7 days.

What the lab works on

Track 1

Evaluation

Twelve years of field evaluation experience encoded into free, open-source browser tools. Six live tools covering 298 indicators across 11 sectors. Sample size calculation, evaluation design advising, data exploration, Theory of Change building, evaluation matrix construction, and an indicator bank. Every tool runs client-side with zero data transmission. The methodology modules work offline in Juba and Ouagadougou, not just in offices with reliable internet.

Track 2

Early Warning

PRAXIS applies epidemiological surveillance logic and econometric methods to ACLED event data to predict escalation in violent extremism. The composite co-occurrence model detects multi-type escalation pathways, and the platform translates those signals into structured alerts for P/CVE programme managers, humanitarian agencies, and security coordination bodies. The Sahel is the proof of concept. 100,184 events across 13 countries confirm the model. Wherever ACLED operates, roughly 230 countries, is the addressable scope.

Who built this

I design and manage evaluations of programmes aimed at preventing violent extremism across Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. Before the Sahel, I ran health systems research in South Sudan and Burundi. My training is in epidemiology. I serve as section editor for case-based evaluations at the Journal of MultiDisciplinary Evaluation.

The conflict early warning work comes from the same gap that produced the evaluation tools. I needed a way to anticipate where VE would escalate so that programme design could get ahead of events instead of reacting to them. I could not find it, so I built it. The prediction model uses the same econometric rigour I would apply to an impact evaluation. The difference is that instead of measuring what happened, it tries to tell you what is about to.
